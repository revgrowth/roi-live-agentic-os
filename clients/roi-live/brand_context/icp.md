# ROI.LIVE — Ideal Customer Profile

## Who They Are

**In one sentence:** Founders and business owners in the $1-3M revenue band (or pre-revenue e-commerce with growth ambition) who've already tried the obvious playbook, watched it fail to move closed revenue, and are now looking for someone to hold both the CMO seat and the execution line — with proof they can interrogate by client name and date.

**Situation:** Company is profitable or near-profitable, product-market fit is directionally there, but growth is choppy. Founder has either (a) hired a fractional CMO who wrote a plan and handed execution to an agency that didn't know the plan, (b) hired an agency that runs ads and builds funnels without connecting the work to revenue, or (c) tried to run it themselves and hit a ceiling on bandwidth. The obvious next moves — post more on LinkedIn, run more Google Ads, hire another specialist — have already been tried and have not moved the number.

**Identity:** Owner-operator. Signs payroll. Carries the revenue risk. Knows the customer. Did not come from the agency world. Sees their business as something they built, not a job they hold.

**Role:** Running the P&L. Still making the big commercial decisions — pricing, hiring, channel bets — because the layer of senior marketing judgment doesn't exist on the team yet. Spending a disproportionate share of their week on coordination between specialists who don't share accountability.

**Stage:** Post-founding, pre-scale. Revenue validated (typically $1-3M) but stuck — either flat for 12+ months or growing slower than the unit economics should permit. Early-stage e-commerce is a secondary stage: pre-revenue to $500K with a product working but no repeatable growth system.

## Primary Pain

**The problem:** The strategy and execution have separate owners, and nothing stays aligned. The CMO writes a 90-day plan, the agency runs last month's campaigns, the founder is the only person in the room holding both sides — which means the founder is the bottleneck on every judgment call. Revenue attribution is murky because no single party owns the line from plan → execution → closed deal.

**What it costs them:**
- Weeks lost to coordination overhead between specialists who don't share context
- Campaigns that launch late or with watered-down versions of the strategy because the agency modified it without knowing why
- Founder hours spent being the connective tissue that should be a senior hire's job
- Revenue left in plain sight — offer is wrong, pricing is wrong, the team is pointing at the wrong metric — that nobody on the current roster has the authority to call out
- 12+ months of plateau before the founder admits the model isn't working

**How they describe it:**
- "We have a plan but nobody's running it."
- "The agency is doing things. I'm not sure they're the right things."
- "My marketing person is really strategic but they don't actually *do* anything."
- "I keep buying specialist tools and hiring specialist people and we're still stuck."
- "I don't know what's working and what isn't."
- "Everyone tells me something different."

**Secondary pains:**
- Benchmark blindness: every SaaS tool and agency deck quotes industry averages the founder can't verify against their own pipeline
- Buyer fatigue on "data-driven" and "growth" language — every pitch sounds the same
- Team pointing at vanity metrics (impressions, CTR, follower count) instead of revenue
- Cash conversion drag — the time between a marketing spend and a closed deal is longer than the founder can comfortably finance at current scale

## What They've Tried

| Alternative | Why it didn't work | What they learned |
|------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Solo fractional CMO (strategy only) | Wrote a great plan; handed it to an agency that executed a different version of it; nobody owned the gap | "Strategy without execution is a document nobody reads." |
| Performance marketing agency | Ran ads and built funnels; never held the commercial seat; optimized for agency-side KPIs (ROAS, CPL) instead of closed revenue | "The agency is measuring what makes them look good, not what pays my mortgage." |
| Growth consultant / playbook course | Gave a framework; no operational muscle attached | "Frameworks don't install themselves." |
| SaaS tool stack promising "AI-driven growth" | Produced outputs nobody knew how to action; added operational complexity | "Another dashboard nobody looks at." |
| Hiring a head of marketing in-house | Too expensive at this stage; available talent pool skewed junior; couldn't both set strategy and run channels | "I need a senior judgment for 10 hours a week, not a junior full-time." |
| Jack-of-all-trades freelancer | Couldn't hold enough of the stack at once; work quality inconsistent | "Generalists without depth can't move a multi-million-dollar P&L." |
| Doing it themselves | Hit a bandwidth ceiling; couldn't be in the business and on the business simultaneously | "I'm the bottleneck." |

**Sophistication level:** Intermediate-to-advanced. Has tried at least two of the above. Has a working BS detector for generic growth language. Wants to see mechanism and proof, not slogans.

## Desired Outcome

**What "after" looks like:** One operator holds the CMO seat and owns the agency executing the plan. Revenue growth follows a documented hypothesis chain from strategy → channel → test → outcome. The founder spends 70% less time coordinating marketing and 100% more time on product, sales, and the two or three commercial decisions only the founder can make. The plateau ends — growth resumes at the rate the unit economics should permit. Typical pattern: $1-3M → $5-10M+ within 18-36 months, with attribution the founder can interrogate by month, campaign, and client segment.

**What they'd tell a friend:** "I finally have a marketing operator who's running it, not advising on it. And every time I ask why we're doing something, he shows me the number from a client he ran this test with last quarter."

**The deeper motivation:** Agency and freedom. The founder wants to stop being the bottleneck — not because they don't care, but because their highest-leverage work is elsewhere and they know it. They also want to stop second-guessing marketing decisions. A single trusted operator with receipts removes a whole category of mental overhead.

## Objections

| Objection | What's really behind it | How to address |
|-----------|------------------------|----------------|
| "You don't know my industry." | Fear that cross-industry experience is a euphemism for generalist and shallow. | Show the cross-industry pattern-transfer example directly: "Here's a conversion principle that worked for an oracle deck brand at $1.2M and we applied it to a pool builder at $4.3M. Here's the math from both." |
| "Isn't fractional CMO just part-time consulting?" | Anchored on the solo-CMO-with-a-90-day-plan model they already tried. | Name the failure mode explicitly. "That's the model that didn't work for you last time. I hold the CMO seat *and* own the team executing against it. Different structure, different outcome." |
| "What's different about your data?" | Suspicion that "proprietary data" is marketing-speak for SaaS-dashboard access. | Offer a specific interrogation. "Name the vertical and a price point. I'll tell you what lead-to-job conversion or AOV looks like at that level, which client I saw it in, and what test produced it." |
| "I already have an agency." | Sunk cost. Relational loyalty. Coordination fatigue about switching. | Don't argue replacement first. Frame the fractional CMO seat as the missing layer. "The agency might be fine. What's missing is someone who owns the plan they're executing against. That's not a replacement — that's the role you've been playing yourself." |
| "What's this going to cost me?" | Budget anchored to either fractional CMO solo rates or agency-only rates; fear of paying twice. | Structural answer first. "One operator, one relationship, one fee. Not a fractional plus an agency stack. Here's the pricing logic against your current spend." |
| "How quickly will I see results?" | Burned by prior engagements that promised 90-day turnarounds. | Honest calibration. "Offer, pricing, and funnel audits surface leaks in the first 30 days. Channel adjustments land inside 60. Compounding growth shows up 6-12 months in, which is the honest timeline for any operator who isn't lying to you." |

## Their Language

**Words they use for the problem:**
- "Stuck" / "plateau" — common framing for the current state
- "My marketing isn't working" — the vague version that hides the specific failure mode
- "I don't know what's working" — attribution blindness
- "Our agency does [X]" — operational description that often signals a gap
- "Shiny objects" — self-aware label for channel-hopping
- "Running the business vs. working in the business" — borrowed from Gerber but real
- "I need someone senior" — the pain point without the exact role clarity

**Words they use for the desired outcome:**
- "Actually move the number"
- "Own it" / "hold the line"
- "Get my time back"
- "Stop second-guessing"
- "Just run it"
- "Attribution I can trust"
- "Grow to [specific revenue target]"

**Words that would turn them off:**
- "Synergize" — category boilerplate
- "Empower" — corporate buzzword
- "Leverage" as verb — AI-telltale
- "Ecosystem" — hollow
- "Journey" (when describing their funnel) — consultant-speak
- "We're excited to partner with you" — vendor tone
- Soft CTAs like "reach out to learn more" — reads as hedging

**Jargon level:** Moderate. They understand ROAS, CAC, LTV, MER, CTR, conversion rate. They do not want to be condescended to. They also do not want to be drowned in acronyms — every technical term used should be in service of a specific decision.

## Buying Triggers

What pushes them from "considering" to "buying":
- A failed quarter that makes the plateau undeniable — revenue flat or down while marketing spend was up
- A marketing hire or agency relationship ending (fired, quit, or mutually phased out)
- A specific Jason Spencer post or Signal article that names the failure mode they're living in and shows a client who escaped it with numbers attached
- A peer founder referral with a named client outcome
- Hitting a revenue threshold where the cost of the current marketing model becomes too expensive to ignore (typically $2M+ for local service; $1M+ for e-commerce)
- A competitor getting visible traction in a channel they're underperforming in
- Running the specific cross-industry pattern-transfer demo with Jason on an intro call — when Jason pulls a client from an unrelated vertical and shows the math that applies to their situation, the conversation usually converts inside the next two calls

## Secondary Segments

Three distinct ICP segments sit under the primary profile. Each has real variation in pain, language, and buying behavior.

### Segment: E-commerce founders ($0–$3M, scaling toward 7 figures and beyond)
- **Who:** Direct-to-consumer brand owners. Product exists, AOV is known, but growth has been unlock-by-unlock (paid channel works until it doesn't, email works until it doesn't). Jason has taken several of these from startup-phase / $0 to 7-figure+.
- **Key difference from primary:** Shorter sales cycle, more acute channel-by-channel performance anxiety, typically running Shopify stack. Offer/pricing/AOV testing is the fastest-moving lever. Email and retargeting revenue attribution is the first proof point.
- **Priority:** High. Fastest-converting ICP segment. Case study density is strongest here (East Perry, Blue Tree's product lines, ReMARKable).

### Segment: Local service business owners (home contractors, HVAC, landscaping, pool builders)
- **Who:** Owner-operators running service-delivery businesses in defined geographic markets. Typically $1-5M, trying to reach $5-15M+. Pipeline-heavy, lead-quality-sensitive. Google Business Profile, paid search, and local SEO are the primary acquisition channels.
- **Key difference from primary:** Pipeline-and-appointment-based vs. transaction-based. Lead-to-job conversion is the metric that actually moves the number. Channel mix is narrower (Google, Meta, email / SMS nurture, local SEO, GBP) but depth within each channel is what wins. Jason's proprietary data in these verticals is the strongest differentiator — specific lead-to-job ratios at specific ticket sizes no SaaS tool holds.
- **Priority:** High. Premium verticals (HVAC, pool, premium landscaping) have high LTV per client and strong retainer economics. Coastal Carolina Comfort is the reference case study.

### Segment: B2B founders
- **Who:** Service-delivery or product-led B2B with a deal size in the $10K-$250K range. Sales cycle measured in weeks or months. Growth is bottlenecked on pipeline quality and sales-marketing alignment more than top-of-funnel volume.
- **Key difference from primary:** Longer cycles mean faster iteration on offer/messaging/positioning has higher leverage than channel optimization. LinkedIn, content, and partnerships outweigh paid search. Sales enablement and tight marketing-to-sales handoff often the biggest win.
- **Priority:** Medium. Fewer case studies publicly documented in this segment to date. Growth opportunity for ROI.LIVE positioning.
